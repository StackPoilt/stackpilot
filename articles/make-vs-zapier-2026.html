Overview: Make vs Zapier at a glance

Both Make and Zapier are no-code automation platforms that connect your apps and automate repetitive tasks. But they emerged from very different philosophies:

Zapier was built for simplicity. Founded in 2011, it pioneered the "if this, then that" model of app automation — and it still executes that model better than anyone. The experience is frictionless. You can set up a working automation in under 10 minutes, even on your first day.

Make (formerly Integromat) was built for power. It's a visual programming environment disguised as a no-code tool. Scenarios (Make's term for automations) can branch, loop, handle errors, transform data, and call APIs in ways Zapier simply can't match — at a price point that's 3–10× lower for equivalent usage.

The short version: Zapier is the on-ramp. Make is the highway.

Ease of use

Zapier wins here, unambiguously. Its linear interface — pick a trigger, add actions, turn it on — is impossible to confuse. There's no new mental model to learn. If you understand "when X happens, do Y," you can build in Zapier on day one.

Make's canvas interface is powerful but initially disorienting. Connecting modules, understanding operations vs. executions, and debugging data flow takes real time investment. Most users need 2–4 hours of learning before they're comfortable. The payoff is enormous, but the ramp exists.

Our recommendation: If someone on your team has built anything in Airtable automations, Google Apps Script, or even Excel macros, they'll pick up Make quickly. If your team is 100% non-technical, start with Zapier and migrate later if pricing becomes a pain.

Integrations

Zapier has 6,000+ app integrations to Make's 1,000+. In practice, this rarely matters — both cover every major business app: Google Workspace, Slack, HubSpot, Salesforce, Stripe, Shopify, Notion, Airtable, and hundreds more.

The gap shows up in niche apps. If your business uses a specialized industry tool (a specific restaurant POS, a niche healthcare platform, a local ERP), Zapier is more likely to have it natively. Make can often connect via webhooks or its HTTP module, but it requires a bit more setup.

AI capabilities in 2026

Both platforms have added robust AI capabilities, but Make has integrated more deeply at the scenario level.

Make's AI module lets you call OpenAI, Anthropic (Claude), Google Gemini, and other LLMs directly in any automation. You can pass email content to Claude, get a classification back, and route the email based on sentiment — all in one scenario, with no code.

Zapier's AI includes an OpenAI integration and a built-in "AI by Zapier" action that can generate text, classify, and summarize. It's slightly less flexible but just as capable for most common AI automation patterns.

For AI-heavy workflows — automatically triaging support tickets, enriching leads with research, generating personalized emails at scale — Make's multi-module approach gives you more control over data flow and error handling.

Best use cases for each

Use Make for:

  • AI-powered lead enrichment and scoring pipelines
  • Complex multi-branch customer onboarding flows
  • Invoice processing (extract data → validate → route → archive)
  • High-volume e-commerce order processing
  • Data sync between multiple systems with transformation
  • Any workflow that processes 10,000+ operations/month

Use Zapier for:

  • Simple CRM → email trigger automations
  • Form submissions → notify team in Slack
  • New lead → create deal in HubSpot
  • Calendar event → Zoom meeting created
  • Any single-trigger, single-action workflow
  • Teams that want zero learning curve

Final verdict: Make wins on value, Zapier wins on simplicity

If we had to pick one tool for a growing small business, we'd choose Make. The power-to-price ratio is unmatched. Once you invest the time to learn it, you'll build automations that would be prohibitively expensive on Zapier — and you'll have the AI integration flexibility to build serious workflows.

But Zapier is the right starting point if you've never automated anything before, you need to move fast, or your team is purely non-technical. You can always migrate to Make once you've validated that automation is worth investing in.

The good news: both have free plans. Start both trials and build the same automation in each — you'll know within an hour which one fits your brain.
